# Best Beaches in Corfu: The 12 You Should Not Miss

**Subtitle:** Corfu's coastline runs for 217 kilometres. Of those 217 kilometres, roughly half is accessible, swimmable, and rewarding — the west coast delivers the most dramatic scenery and the finest sandy beaches; the north has long sandy arcs ideal for families; the east has calm, shallow water; and scattered throughout are coves that require hiking, boat, or local knowledge to reach and reward all three with extraordinary water clarity. These are the twelve that justify the effort.

**Excerpt:** One rule before we begin: west coast beaches in Corfu are mostly sandy, east coast beaches are mostly pebbly. Neither is a compromise — the pebble beaches of the northeast have some of the most dramatic settings and clearest water on the island. But if you need sand under your feet, the west coast is where you want to be, and Glyfada and Agios Gordios are your primary destinations.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Arrive early or arrive late.** The popular beaches — Paleokastritsa, Glyfada, Canal d'Amour — are significantly more enjoyable before 10am and after 5pm. The light in the early morning and late afternoon is also better for photography and the atmosphere is genuinely different without the mid-day crowds.
2. **The hike-in beaches are worth the effort.** Porto Timoni requires a 30-minute walk down from the Afionas village parking area. The reward is a double beach with no vehicles, no facilities, and the clearest water on the west coast. Rovinia is similar. The physical effort is a filter that improves the beach.
3. **Beach clubs are concentrated on the west and south coast.** Glyfada and Agios Gordios have the most developed beach club scenes — sun loungers, DJs, cocktail bars integrated into the beach experience. If that is what you are looking for, these are your beaches.
4. **Bring water shoes for east coast beaches.** Many northeast coast beaches have rocky seabeds that make water entry uncomfortable without protection. Barbati and Kassiopi are beautiful but rocky underfoot; water shoes make a genuine difference and are easy to buy locally.
5. **A car or scooter is strongly recommended.** Bus service covers the main destinations but with limited frequency. The finest beaches on the west coast are 45–90 minutes from Corfu Town by public transport; 25–45 minutes by car.

## ARTICLE BODY

Corfu's beaches are the island's primary attraction, and depending on which part of the coastline you are on, they are almost entirely different experiences. The island is mountainous through its centre — the peak of Mount Pantokrator reaches 906 metres — and the coastal character reflects the geology on either side of that spine.

The west coast faces the open Ionian Sea. The beaches here are exposed, sandy, and dramatically framed by limestone cliffs and pine-covered hills. The water is marginally cooler and occasionally windier than the east, but the scenery is exceptional and the sunsets are the best on the island.

The east coast faces Albania and the Greek mainland across a narrow channel. The beaches here are calmer, shallower, and more sheltered — water temperatures marginally warmer, no surf, ideal for children. The seabed is typically rocky or pebbly. The atmosphere is less dramatic but more relaxing.

## 1. Paleokastritsa — The Most Beautiful Setting

**Location:** Northwest coast, 24 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Multiple pebble and sand coves

Paleokastritsa is not a single beach but a system of six coves arranged around three main bays, framed by limestone cliffs, forested headlands, and water that ranges from turquoise to deep green depending on depth and time of day. It is the most photographed beach destination in Corfu and it deserves the attention.

The main beach (**Agios Spyridon**) has the most facilities — sunbeds, watersports, restaurants built into the cliff face, and the famous **La Grotta** bar cut into the rock above the water. Above the beach, a 13th-century monastery with magnificent views and a small museum of religious objects. The other coves — **Agia Triada**, **Platakia**, **Alipa** — are accessible on foot or by boat and vary from organised to nearly deserted.

Snorkelling here is the best on the island. The rocky seabed, water clarity, and density of marine life make it worth bringing a mask. Buses run hourly from Corfu Town in summer. By car: 30–35 minutes. The main beach fills by 11am in July–August; arrive before 9:30am or after 5pm.


## 2. Glyfada — The Best Sandy Beach on the West Coast

**Location:** West coast, 17 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Wide sandy beach

Glyfada is a wide, south-facing sandy beach framed by pine-covered cliffs — the most developed and most cosmopolitan beach on the island's west coast. It has everything: multiple **beach clubs** with sun loungers, cocktail bars and DJs, watersports, beach volleyball, and easy access with substantial parking.

The **Corfu beach club** scene here is the most active on the island — several operators run organised sunbed areas with bar service and music (typically house or lounge from midday), which is the right atmosphere for visitors who want organised beach life. The beach itself is 400 metres long and sandy, with progressively deeper water — gentle enough for confident swimmers. The cliff walk south to Kontogialos (30 minutes, spectacular) is one of the best short walks on the west coast.

Bus: Regular service from Corfu Town (Line 5). Car: 25 minutes from town.


## 3. Porto Timoni — The Hiker's Reward

**Location:** Northwest coast, near Afionas village, 33 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Double pebble cove, no facilities

Porto Timoni is a double beach — two small coves separated by a narrow rocky isthmus — accessible only by a 30-minute walk down from the Afionas village parking area or by boat from Agios Georgios Pagi. It has no facilities. Bring everything you need for the day.

The walk is not technically difficult but it is steep in places and slippery after rain. The reward is one of the finest beach environments on the west coast — the water in both coves is exceptionally clear (the rockier seabed is better here than sand for water clarity), the setting is framed by juniper and maquis, and the number of people is self-limited by the walk.

The viewpoint from the Afionas promontory above is one of the best on the island — both coves visible simultaneously, Antipaxos in the distance, and the specific aquamarine of Ionian water in the afternoon light. Arrive at the car park by 8:30am in high season, or after 4pm as the majority of visitors are leaving.


## 4. Canal d'Amour — The Legend

**Location:** North coast, Sidari, 38 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Sandy beach with sandstone rock formations and natural channels

Canal d'Amour is a narrow channel carved through sandstone cliffs by centuries of wave action — a passage of crystal-clear water, perhaps 10 metres wide, between walls of yellow sandstone sculpted into organic forms. The legend: couples who swim through the channel together will soon marry. The swim itself takes about two minutes and is easy enough for older children.

The **geology** is the genuine attraction — the sandstone formations around the main channel are extraordinary, with caves, arches, and passages accessible by swimming or snorkelling at low water. The beach fills quickly in summer; arrive before 10am. **SUP and canoe rental** here is excellent — the surrounding channels and coves are best explored from the water. By car: 45–50 minutes from Corfu Town.


## 5. Agios Gordios — Sand, Cliffs, and Beach Atmosphere

**Location:** West-southwest coast, 17 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Wide sandy beach

Agios Gordios is a long sandy beach enclosed by dramatic cliffs — the southern headland drops vertically into the sea, creating a natural amphitheatre. The beach is wide, sandy, and fully organised with sunbeds, watersports, multiple **Corfu beach clubs**, and restaurants. The **Pink Palace** resort above the beach is famous on the young traveller circuit, and its atmosphere extends to the beach below.

**Watersports:** Parasailing, jet skiing, banana boats, and paddleboard rental all available from the beach. One of the best-equipped beaches on the island for active water activities. The west-facing aspect makes Agios Gordios one of the finest sunset beaches on the island.


## 6. Myrtiotissa — The Hidden Nudist Cove

**Location:** West coast, 18 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Sandy and pebble cove, minimal facilities

Lawrence Durrell called Myrtiotissa "perhaps the most beautiful beach in the world" — an endorsement cited so many times it has become its own cliché, but the beach does not disappoint the first time. It sits at the base of a steep cliff at the end of a difficult access road (only small vehicles can complete the final descent; most visitors park at the top and walk 15–20 minutes down). The beach is small, mixed sand and pebble, and clothing-optional.

The water clarity at Myrtiotissa is exceptional — the combination of a rocky seabed, no river outflow, and protected position creates conditions that snorkellers find remarkable. A small canteen provides drinks and basic food. Access also by boat from Paleokastritsa in summer.


## 7. Barbati — Pebbles, Mountains, and the Northeast's Finest Water

**Location:** Northeast coast, 28 km north of Corfu Town | **Type:** Pebble beach

Barbati is the northeast coast beach most consistently recommended for water quality and setting — white pebbles at the foot of the Pantokrator massif, extraordinarily clear water, and enough sunbed and restaurant infrastructure to be comfortable without losing the authentic character of the northeast's lower-key coastline.

The water is the feature: the sheltered position, pebble (not sand) seabed, and absence of river input produce the clarity typical of the northeast coast. Snorkelling in the rocky sections either side of the main beach is excellent. The 3 km coastal walk from Ipsos to Barbati is one of the better short walks on the east coast.


## 8. Kassiopi — Village, History, and the Best of the Far Northeast

**Location:** Northeast coast, 36 km north of Corfu Town | **Type:** Multiple small pebble beaches around the village

Kassiopi is a real fishing village that happens to have some of the best swimming on the northeast coast — the small beaches around the village's rocky headland (Bataria, Kalamionas, Kanoni) have the clearest water of any organised beach in this part of Corfu. The village itself — a Byzantine castle, a Roman-era temple site, excellent fish tavernas on the harbour, and a diving school — provides more context than a purely beach destination.

**Bataria Beach** (pebble, well-sheltered, excellent for families with young children) and **Kalamionas** (more exposed, better for snorkelling) are the main options. **Diving:** Kassiopi is the best base for scuba diving on the northeast coast, with the rocky seabed, underwater caves, and wall dives near the headland attracting both beginners and experienced divers.


## 9. Agios Georgios (North) — The Long Sandy Arc

**Location:** Northwest coast, 35 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Long sandy beach

Agios Georgios north — distinguished from the Agios Gordios in the southwest — is a 3-km arc of sand-and-pebble beach facing west, with calm, shallow water perfect for families and a range of facilities from beach bars to boat rental. It is significantly less crowded than the more famous west coast beaches and has genuine sunset views.

The adjacent Porto Timoni (a 30-minute walk from the beach) makes Agios Georgios a practical base for combining organised beach time with the hike-in experience.


## 10. Rovinia — The Secret Cove

**Location:** West coast, near Liapades village, 28 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Small pebble cove, minimal facilities

Rovinia is the kind of beach that appears in conversations among people who have been to Corfu more than once. A small pebble cove below the Liapades road, accessible by a 10-minute walk or by boat from Paleokastritsa. A small canteen provides drinks. A natural cave on the south end of the beach is swimmable at low water.

The water clarity is exceptional — deeper and cooler than the sandy west coast beaches, with a rocky seabed that rewards snorkelling. Less busy than Paleokastritsa, more accessible than Porto Timoni.


## 11. Arillas — The Family Beach of the Northwest

**Location:** Northwest coast, 45 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Long sandy beach

Arillas is protected by two small peninsulas that create calm, shallow water ideal for families with young children. The beach is one of the longer sandy beaches in Corfu, and the small village behind it has enough tavernas and accommodation to make it a self-contained destination for a week. Substantially less developed and less crowded than Glyfada or Agios Gordios, with a more traditional Corfu character.


## 12. Halikounas / Lake Korission — Kitesurfing and Wild Coast

**Location:** South coast, 44 km from Corfu Town | **Type:** Long sandy beach backed by a lagoon, minimal facilities

Halikounas is the most unusual beach in this list — a 4-km strip of sand separating the Ionian Sea from the **Lake Korission** lagoon nature reserve, with strong winds that make it the best kitesurfing and windsurfing location in the Ionian, and a character that is wilder and more natural than anything else in Corfu.

The **Ionian Kiteboarding** and **Free Spirit Surf Camp** schools operate here — this is the correct beach for kitesurfing or windsurfing lessons. For everyone else, it is a beach for long walks, birdwatching (the lagoon is an important migratory habitat), and the pleasure of a nearly empty shoreline.


## Corfu Beach Clubs: Where to Find Them

The **Corfu beach club** scene is concentrated at a few beaches. **Glyfada** has the most active beach club operations — multiple operators run organised sunbed areas with cocktail bar service and music (typically house or lounge from midday). **Agios Gordios** has a lively beach atmosphere with the Pink Palace influence. **Kavos** in the far south is the full party resort experience — loud, late, and specifically aimed at young visitors who want exactly that.

The northeast coast (Dassia, Ipsos, Gouvia) has organised beach bars but not beach clubs in the Glyfada sense — more taverna-adjacent than DJ-oriented. For Mykonos-style ultra-luxury beach clubs, Corfu is not the right destination — the scene here is more relaxed and genuinely accessible.


## Practical Corfu Beach Guide

**Getting around:** Car or scooter is the most practical option for beach-hopping. Blue buses (KTEL) serve the main destinations — Glyfada, Paleokastritsa, Agios Gordios, Sidari, Kassiopi — but with limited frequency. Boat trips from Corfu Town reach the northwest coast inaccessible coves.

**What to bring:** Water shoes for rocky east coast beaches; mask and snorkel for multiple beaches (Paleokastritsa, Rovinia, Kassiopi area reward underwater exploration); full supplies for no-facility beaches — Porto Timoni has nothing.

**Blue Flag beaches:** Corfu has approximately 30 Blue Flag beaches — among the highest concentrations in Greece. The designation indicates water quality and management standards rather than scenery or suitability for specific visitors.

**Key tip on timing:** Arrive at the most popular beaches (Paleokastritsa, Glyfada, Canal d'Amour) before 10am in July–August to secure your spot. Alternatively, arrive after 5pm — the light is better and the crowds have thinned significantly.
