# Best Hotels in Spetses, Greece: Our Top Picks for 2026

Spetses is the Greek island that dresses for dinner. Not literally — though you'll see it happen at the Poseidonion's terrace restaurant — but in the sense that the island carries itself with a certain composure, a certain awareness of its own elegance, that most Greek islands either never had or long ago traded for tourism revenue. The harbor is lined with neoclassical mansions that were built by shipping families in the 18th and 19th centuries, their proportions and details announcing a prosperity rooted in the sea. The waterfront promenade is shaded by pine trees. Horse-drawn carriages move along the quay at a pace that makes the modern concept of efficiency seem both recent and unnecessary.

The Athenian connection runs deep. Wealthy Athenian families began summering on Spetses in the early 20th century, and the Poseidonion Grand Hotel — opened in 1914, modeled on the Côte d'Azur palaces, recently and beautifully restored — became the social anchor. That clientele brought expectations: good food, architectural taste, cultural events, and a kind of social polish that permeated the island. Spetses today retains this character — it's the Greek island where you're as likely to see an art exhibition as a beach bar.

The car-free policy in the main town (private vehicles are banned; scooters and bicycles are allowed) creates an atmosphere that's quieter than most islands — though not as radically silent as [Hydra](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/hydra-travel-guide), where all motorized transport is prohibited. The scooters buzz, but the overall pace is set by the clip-clop of the carriages, the rhythm of walking, and the gentle awareness that nothing on Spetses is genuinely urgent.

## Luxury & Grand Hotels

### Poseidonion Grand Hotel

The most iconic hotel on Spetses — and one of the most historically significant in Greece. The Poseidonion was built in 1914 by Sotirios Anargiros (a Spetses-born benefactor who modeled it on the grand hotels of the French Riviera), fell into decline through the mid-20th century, and was magnificently restored in 2009 to a standard that honors the original vision while adding contemporary comfort and a world-class spa.

The building itself is the attraction: a neoclassical palace facing the harbor, with grand public spaces, a sweeping staircase, and the kind of architectural confidence that only comes from an era when hotels were built as monuments. The rooms are elegant — period furnishings, high ceilings, marble bathrooms. The spa is excellent. The terrace restaurant, overlooking the harbor square and the sea, is the island's most sophisticated dining experience.

The Poseidonion is the hotel that anchors Spetses's identity. Staying here is participating in the island's history — and the harbor-front terrace at sunset, with a glass of wine and the horse-drawn carriages passing below, is one of the most civilized moments available in the Greek islands.

**Price range:** €250–600/night
**Best for:** Luxury seekers, history lovers, couples celebrating something important, anyone wanting the island's most iconic experience
**Good to know:** The harbor-front rooms have the best views. The spa is worth a full session, not just a quick treatment. The restaurant is excellent but expensive — the harbor's other restaurants are steps away for more casual evenings. Book well ahead for July–August weekends.

### Nissia Traditional Residences

A neoclassical estate — several restored buildings set in walled gardens with a pool, elegant rooms, and the atmosphere of a private Spetses compound rather than a hotel. The architecture preserves the mansion heritage while the interiors have been updated with contemporary taste. The pool, set in the garden, provides the retreat space that the island's car-free streets don't always offer in summer heat.

**Price range:** €180–400/night
**Best for:** Couples, families wanting elegant space, anyone who values garden privacy and architectural character
**Good to know:** The estate setting means more space and privacy than the harbor-front hotels. The harbor is a short walk. The pool is a genuine advantage — most Spetses hotels don't have one.

## Boutique & Mansion Hotels

### Orloff Resort

A restored 18th-century mansion — named after the Russian count whose fleet briefly liberated Spetses during the Russo-Turkish War — with intimate rooms, a walled garden courtyard, and an atmosphere of cultivated elegance. The building's history shows in the stone walls, the wooden ceilings, and the proportions of rooms designed for a grander era. The garden is the private retreat — shaded, quiet, and beautifully maintained.

**Price range:** €150–320/night
**Best for:** Romantic couples, architecture enthusiasts, anyone wanting intimate neoclassical character
**Good to know:** No pool — the garden is the communal space. The harbor is a short walk. The rooms are individually designed; the superior categories have more space and better light.

### Economou Mansion

A neoclassical guesthouse near the harbor with rooms that preserve period details — painted ceilings, wooden floors, wrought-iron balconies — at prices that make Spetses's architectural heritage accessible to mid-range budgets. The building is charming without being precious, and the family hospitality adds warmth to the historical setting.

**Price range:** €90–200/night
**Best for:** Mid-range seekers wanting neoclassical character, couples, practical travelers
**Good to know:** Rooms vary — the best have original ceiling paintings and harbor glimpses. No pool. The harbor's restaurants and cafés are a short walk. Breakfast features local products.

## Practical Tips for Spetses Hotels

**Getting there.** Hydrofoil or catamaran from Piraeus: about 2–2.5 hours. Water taxi from Costa (on the Peloponnese coast, across the strait): about 10 minutes — the fastest route if you're driving from [Athens](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/where-to-stay-in-athens) or the Peloponnese. No airport. Spetses also connects to [Hydra](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/hydra-travel-guide) and [Poros](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/poros-travel-guide) by ferry.

**Getting around.** The town center is car-free — walk, bicycle, or take a horse-drawn carriage. Scooters are allowed and popular. The island's beaches (Agia Paraskevi, Agioi Anargyroi) are reached by water taxi from the harbor (about 15–20 minutes), by the island bus, or by bicycle along the coast road. The island is about 13 km in circumference — a bicycle ride around it takes 1–2 hours.

**The beaches.** Agia Paraskevi (south coast) is the most popular — sandy, organized, pine-backed, with a couple of good tavernas. Agioi Anargyroi (southwest) is a longer sandy beach, also pine-backed. Both are reached by water taxi or bus. Zogeria (west) is wilder and pebbly. The water around Spetses is clean and warm.

**When to visit.** June and September are ideal. Spetses fills with Athenian weekenders in July–August — prices peak and hotels sell out. The Armata Festival (September, commemorating the 1822 naval battle) features a re-enactment with a burning ship in the harbor — spectacular if you time it right. May and October are pleasant. From Spetses, the water taxi to Costa connects to the Peloponnese for [Nafplio](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/nafplio-travel-guide), [Monemvasia](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/monemvasia-travel-guide), and [Epidaurus](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/epidaurus-travel-guide).
