# Best Hotels in Aegina, Greece: Our Top Picks for 2026

Aegina is the Greek island hiding in plain sight. One hour from Athens by ferry — forty minutes by hydrofoil — and yet most international visitors sail right past it on their way to Santorini or Mykonos. This is a mistake, and an increasingly recognized one.

Aegina has one of the best-preserved ancient Greek temples in existence (the Temple of Aphaia), a charming neoclassical port town, Greece's most famous pistachio groves, and a coastline of small beaches and fishing villages that feels genuinely lived-in rather than curated for tourism. The island was Greece's first capital in 1828, and the grand neoclassical buildings along the harbor still carry that brief moment of national importance in their facades.

What makes Aegina special for visitors isn't the monuments or the beaches alone — it's the proximity to Athens combined with the authenticity. You can have breakfast in [Koukaki](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/where-to-stay-in-athens), catch a midday ferry, and be eating pistachios on the Aegina harbor by early afternoon. The island is close enough to Athens to feel like an extension of the city's weekend life, and that proximity means the restaurants, cafés, and hotels cater to Athenians first and tourists second. The result is an island with genuine quality and no tourist-trap nonsense.

The hotel scene is small and personal. Aegina doesn't have five-star resorts or luxury chains — what it has is a handful of characterful boutiques, restored neoclassical buildings, and family-run hotels where the owners know every guest. For travelers who find the Cyclades overcrowded and overpriced, Aegina offers a different kind of Greek island experience — one that's quieter, more affordable, and just as beautiful in its own understated way.

## Boutique & Character Hotels

### Rastoni Guesthouse

The most refined accommodation on Aegina — a neoclassical building on the harbor restored into a small guesthouse with individually designed rooms, original architectural details, and a rooftop terrace with views across the Saronic Gulf. Breakfast is served in a garden courtyard with homemade pastries, local cheese, and Aegina's pistachio products. The building has the kind of quiet elegance that comes from a thoughtful restoration rather than a big-budget build.

The location is ideal: directly on the harbor, steps from the cafés, the fish market, and the ferry dock. The harbor at Aegina is one of the most pleasant in the Saronic — fishing boats alongside cafés, the scent of grilled octopus in the evening air, and the neoclassical facades turning pink at sunset.

**Price range:** €100–220/night
**Best for:** Couples, architecture lovers, travelers wanting the most atmospheric stay on the island
**Good to know:** Small property with limited rooms — book ahead for summer weekends (Athenians fill Aegina from Friday evening). The harbor-front location is lively in the evening — a feature for most, noise for a few.

[Check prices for Rastoni Guesthouse on Booking.com](https://www.booking.com/hotel/gr/rastoni.html)

### Aeginitic Archontiko

A restored traditional mansion set on a hillside above Aegina Town, with views across the rooftops to the harbor and the Saronic Gulf. The interiors blend period architecture — stone walls, wooden beams, arched doorways — with comfortable modern furnishing. The atmosphere is intimate and personal, the kind of hotel where the owner greets you with coffee and pistachio cake and the conversation drifts into island stories.

The hilltop position means a short uphill walk from the harbor, which provides both views and quiet. The rooms have a warmth and individuality that chain hotels can never replicate. Breakfast is homemade and generous.

**Price range:** €80–180/night
**Best for:** Couples seeking romance and quiet, travelers who appreciate traditional architecture, repeat Saronic visitors
**Good to know:** The hillside location means uphill walking — a consideration in summer heat. The views compensate. The property is small; book ahead for weekends.

[Check prices for Aeginitic Archontiko on Booking.com](https://www.booking.com/hotel/gr/aeginitic-archontiko.html)

### Elia Guesthouse

A minimal, design-conscious guesthouse near the harbor — the kind of property that signals Aegina's evolution from a sleepy Saronic island to a weekend destination for Athens's creative class. Rooms are clean-lined and thoughtfully designed, with quality beds, natural materials, and the kind of pared-back aesthetic that lets the island outside the window do the talking.

Elia is the stay for travelers who don't want fuss or frills — just a well-designed room, a good location, and an honest price. The harbor cafés and restaurants are a two-minute walk.

**Price range:** €90–190/night
**Best for:** Design-conscious travelers, couples, solo visitors, anyone wanting a modern aesthetic in a traditional setting
**Good to know:** No pool, no garden, no restaurant. The island itself is the amenity. The harbor's restaurants and the island's beaches are your daytime agenda.

## Harbor-Front & Practical Hotels

### Aegina Hotel

Maximum convenience: a hotel steps from the ferry dock with harbor-front rooms and views across to the Peloponnese mountains. This is the hotel for travelers who value logistics — arriving by ferry and walking directly to your room, stepping out in the morning with the harbor at your feet, catching a late ferry back to Athens without stress.

Rooms are clean and comfortable, recently updated, with the kind of reliable quality that practical travelers appreciate. The harbor is literally at your door. The fish tavernas are a one-minute walk. The pistachio shops are across the quay.

**Price range:** €70–160/night
**Best for:** Travelers arriving by ferry, practical visitors, anyone wanting maximum harbor convenience
**Good to know:** Harbor-front rooms have views but catch some dock noise (ferries, early-morning fishing boats). Rear rooms are quieter. The price-to-location ratio is excellent.

## Beach Hotels

### Hotel Apollo (Agia Marina)

The main beach resort on Aegina — set on Agia Marina, the island's longest sandy beach on the east coast. The hotel has a pool, family rooms, a restaurant, and direct beach access. Agia Marina is the beach that families choose: shallow, sandy, organized with sunbeds, and backed by a small village with tavernas and shops.

Hotel Apollo is not a luxury property. It's a well-run, comfortable, beach-focused hotel that does what families need it to do — provide a clean base with a pool and a beach, in a location where children can swim safely and parents can relax. The Temple of Aphaia is a fifteen-minute drive uphill from Agia Marina, which makes combining beach days with ancient history seamless.

**Price range:** €70–160/night
**Best for:** Families, beach lovers, travelers wanting a different pace from Aegina Town
**Good to know:** Agia Marina is more tourist-oriented than Aegina Town — the tavernas are fine but less characterful. The bus to Aegina Town runs regularly. A car opens up the island's quieter western coast.

## Budget Hotels Worth Booking

### Hotel Plaza

A clean, simple hotel in the center of Aegina Town — a two-minute walk from the harbor and the ferry dock. Rooms are basic but well-maintained: air-conditioned, comfortable beds, functional bathrooms. No pool, no garden, no rooftop — just an honest room at an honest price in the middle of everything.

Aegina is already an affordable island. Staying at Hotel Plaza makes it even more so — and the money you save funds the experiences that make the island rewarding: a fish lunch at the harbor, a pistachio-tasting tour, the boat to the hidden swimming spot at Moni island, an afternoon at the Temple of Aphaia.

**Price range:** €45–100/night
**Best for:** Budget travelers, solo visitors, weekend trippers from Athens, anyone who prioritizes spending on food and experiences
**Good to know:** Basic rooms — manage expectations. The central location and honest price are the selling points. Book ahead for summer weekends when Athenians descend.

## Practical Tips for Aegina Hotels

**Getting there.** Ferries from Athens's Piraeus port take about 70 minutes (conventional) or 40 minutes (hydrofoil/Flying Dolphin). Departures are frequent — roughly hourly in summer. No advance booking needed for most ferries; just buy a ticket at the port. The proximity to Athens makes Aegina possible as a day trip, though an overnight stay reveals the island's evening and morning charm.

**Day trip or overnight?** Overnight. Aegina is doable as a day trip from [Athens](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/where-to-stay-in-athens), but the island's best moments happen in the evening — when the day-trippers leave, the harbor settles into its local rhythm, and the tavernas fill with Athenians rather than cruise passengers. Two nights is ideal.

**The Temple of Aphaia.** One of the best-preserved Doric temples in Greece — remarkably intact, beautifully positioned on a pine-covered hilltop with views to both Athens and the Peloponnese. Most international visitors to Aegina never see it. Take a taxi or rent a car; it's about twenty minutes from Aegina Town. [Book a guided experience on GetYourGuide](https://www.getyourguide.com/athens-l91/athens-aegina-island-small-group-premium-guided-tour-t909841/).

**Pistachios.** Aegina pistachios are famous throughout Greece — the island's volcanic soil and microclimate produce a nut that's distinctly different from (and, Greeks will tell you, superior to) any other pistachio in the world. Buy them at the harbor-front shops in Aegina Town. Try the pistachio ice cream at any of the harbor gelaterias. The pistachio-studded pastries at local bakeries are exceptional.

**Weekend timing.** Aegina is an Athenian weekend escape. Hotels fill from Friday afternoon through Sunday. If you're visiting on a weekend, book ahead. Midweek visits are quieter and cheaper.

**Combining with other Saronic Islands.** Aegina pairs naturally with [Hydra](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/hydra-travel-guide) (car-free island, about 2 hours by ferry from Aegina) and [Poros](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/poros-travel-guide) and [Spetses](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/spetses-travel-guide). A Saronic Islands hop is an excellent short addition to an [Athens stay](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/3-days-in-athens) — closer, cheaper, and less logistically complex than the Cyclades.
