## ARTICLE BODY

Let's deal with the question you came here for, because most pages bury it: **no, you cannot go on Shipwreck Beach in 2026.**

Not on a tour, not with a private boat, not by swimming from a boat anchored nearby. The beach at Navagio — the white sand cove on the northwest coast of Zakynthos with the rusted hull of the MV Panagiotis sitting in the middle of it — is closed to landings, and has been since 2022. In June 2026 the Greek government extended that closure through **31 October 2026**.

Here's what that means in practice. You can't walk on the sand. You can't swim in the bay. You can't touch the wreck, photograph yourself next to it, or climb on it. Boats are required to hold roughly 50 metres offshore, so even the "swim from the boat" workaround is out.

What you *can* do is see it — from the clifftop viewpoint above, from a boat holding position at the mouth of the cove, or both in the same morning. And that's still one of the better half-days in the Ionian. It just isn't the half-day the photographs sold you.

The reason this guide exists is that **an enormous amount of what's online about Navagio is out of date.** Tour listings on the major booking platforms still say things like "walk on the sandy beach and explore the ship." Travel blogs still describe a leisurely hour on the sand. Some of those pages were updated in 2026 and are still wrong. We'd rather you found out here than at Porto Vromi harbour.


## Why Is Shipwreck Beach Closed? The Short History

Navagio sits inside a steep, enclosed bay ringed by limestone cliffs on three sides. That geometry is exactly what makes the photograph so extraordinary — and exactly what makes the site dangerous. Rock falls into a confined space with nowhere for people on the sand to go.

The closures have come in waves rather than as one decision:

**2018 — the first collapse.** A section of cliff face broke away and fell onto the beach, injuring seven people and generating a localised wave in the bay. Footage of the collapse circulated widely and put the site's stability into international headlines. The bay closed while geologists assessed it, then reopened.

**September 2022 — the earthquake.** A magnitude 5.4 earthquake struck the water between Zakynthos and Kefalonia, bringing down a large section of cliff. OASP classified the bay as unsafe for landings, and walking and swimming at the beach were banned. This is the closure that has never been lifted.

**June 2026 — the extension.** After a tourist fell from the cliff near the viewpoint and was seriously injured, a fresh risk assessment followed and the ban was carried through to 31 October 2026 by joint ministerial decision. Barriers were placed across access routes and the maritime exclusion zone was reiterated.

There is also a **restoration project** in the works: authorities have approved a plan to add roughly 45,000 cubic metres of gravel to extend the beach around 30 metres seaward, with the aim of moving the wreck further from the water and slowing its erosion. No reopening date has been tied to it.

> ⚠️ **The honest position on reopening:** nobody can tell you when — or whether — landings will resume. The restriction has been reviewed and renewed repeatedly since 2022, and the authorities have said they reassess roughly twice a year. Plan your trip around the site being closed. If it reopens while you're there, treat it as a bonus.

The important nuance: **the site isn't sealed off — the *beach* is.** Plenty of people assume the closure means there's no point going north at all. That's wrong, and it's why so many visitors either skip the best view on the island or turn up expecting the wrong thing.


## Option 1: The Clifftop Viewpoint (Free, and It's the Famous Shot)

Here's the thing worth internalising: **the photograph you have in your head was taken from the viewpoint, not from the beach.** The aerial composition — wreck small and central, cliffs framing, water graduating from white through turquoise to deep blue — is only possible from above. Visitors who only take the boat often come away slightly puzzled that it didn't look like the pictures. It doesn't, from sea level.

**Where it is.** The platform sits on the cliff above **Anafonitria** village on the northwest of the island, roughly at 37.86°N, 20.62°E. Follow signs for Anafonitria, then for the **Navagio Viewpoint** — some signage also uses "Myzithres."

**Getting there.** Around **40–50 minutes by car from Zakynthos Town**, roughly 25 minutes from Tsilivi. The road is paved the whole way but narrow in places, with a few tight bends through the villages. There's a parking area at the top; reports vary between free and a small charge of €2–3, so carry a couple of euros. Entry to the platform itself is free.

**What you'll find.** A short walk of a few minutes from the parking area to the main viewing platform, which has a reinforced safety railing. There are usually a kiosk selling cold drinks and basic toilets. Several smaller informal lookouts sit along the path — often better for photographs, because the main platform gets a queue.

**When to go.** Aim for **9–11am**: the sun is on the cliffs and the sand, the boats haven't clustered below yet, and the queue is manageable. In July and August, expect a 10–15 minute wait at the main platform after 11am. Late afternoon, roughly an hour before sunset, is the other good window and gives warmer light on the limestone.

**A safety note that isn't boilerplate.** People have died at this viewpoint. The cliff edge is unstable, the wind on the exposed clifftop is strong enough to take a hat off your head, and the June 2026 closure extension was triggered partly by a tourist falling. Stay behind the railings. Don't climb over for a better angle — the better angle is fifty metres along the path anyway.


## Option 2: The Boat Trip (Still Worth It, With Adjusted Expectations)

Boat tours to Navagio run all season and remain genuinely worthwhile — you get the scale of the cliffs from below, which the viewpoint can't convey, and most trips combine the wreck with sea caves and swimming stops elsewhere.

What's changed is the stop itself. Boats now hold position offshore rather than landing. Expect a **photo stop of 10–20 minutes** at the mouth of the cove rather than an hour on the sand, and expect your swimming to happen somewhere else on the itinerary.

There are three sensible departure points, and the choice matters more than the operator.

### Porto Vromi — closest and fastest

A small working harbour on the west coast, reached by driving to Anafonitria then descending a steep road to the water. Boats reach Navagio in **15–20 minutes**, which means less time in transit and more time at the wreck. The boats are smaller, the ride is calmer, and it's what islanders generally choose. Trips are short — typically 1.5–3 hours with a swim stop or two.

Note that Porto Vromi is a harbour, not a resort: there's very little there beyond the boats and parking, which fills by mid-morning in peak season.

### Agios Nikolaos — pair it with the Blue Caves

In the far north of the island. Boats from here run out to the **Blue Caves at Cape Skinari** first, then south to Navagio — a total of roughly 3–4 hours. This is the format we'd recommend for most visitors, because the Blue Caves are entirely unaffected by the closure and are the best in-water experience on this coast. The sea light inside the caves turns a shade of blue that genuinely doesn't photograph accurately.

### Zakynthos Town — the full day cruise

Large day boats leave the main port for **6–8 hour** circuits with lunch included, multiple swim stops, and sometimes a turtle-spotting attempt in Laganas Bay on the way. Fine if you want a whole day on the water with food handled. Less intimate, more expensive, and a lot of the time is transit.

## How to Get the Famous Aerial Shot (Without a Drone)

A surprising share of people searching for Navagio are looking for the aerial image specifically — and there's a trap in that.

**Drones are not permitted over the site without special authorisation, and fines are applied.** Given the site's safety status and its protected surroundings, this is enforced. Don't plan on flying one.

The good news is that you don't need to. The clifftop viewpoint sits high enough above the cove that a standard phone camera produces the classic composition — this is where essentially every famous Navagio photograph comes from. A few things that actually help:

- **Go between 9 and 11am.** The sun is on the sand and the cliff faces; later in the day the cove falls into shadow and the turquoise flattens out.
- **Walk past the main platform.** The informal lookouts along the path give you cleaner foreground and no queue.
- **Wide angle, and don't zoom.** The drama is the enclosure — cliffs on three sides — not the ship itself. Zooming in on the wreck loses the whole point of the image.
- **Bring a hat you can hold onto.** The wind up there is significant, and it's the main reason phone shots come out blurred.

## Where to Swim Instead

Since Navagio itself is look-only, the swimming has to happen elsewhere — and the coast around it delivers. Most boat itineraries include one or two of these anyway:

**Xygia Sulphur Beach.** Natural sulphur springs seep out under the cliff, giving the water a faint mineral smell and a reputation among locals for being good for the skin. Small, dramatic, and unlike anywhere else on the island.

**Porto Limnionas.** A deep rocky cove on the west coast with exceptionally clear water and a taverna above it. No sand, but it's the best swim on this side of Zakynthos.

**White Beach and the coves near Porto Vromi.** Standard swim stops on the Porto Vromi boat itineraries — clear, sheltered, and reachable only by sea.

**The Blue Caves.** Worth repeating: unaffected by the closure, and you can swim in and around them on most northern boat trips.


## Is Shipwreck Beach Still Worth Visiting in 2026?

Yes — with the expectation reset.

If your plan was to stand on that sand with the hull behind you, that plan isn't available and no operator can make it available. If you can accept seeing it rather than standing on it, the viewpoint is one of the great views in Greece and the boat trip gives you the scale from below. Combined, they make an excellent morning.

Where we'd temper it: **don't build an entire Zakynthos trip around Navagio in its current state.** The island's other headline experience — the loggerhead turtles of the Gulf of Laganas, the largest nesting concentration of the species in the Mediterranean — is fully open and arguably the more distinctive thing about Zakynthos right now. The mountain interior, the Blue Caves, and the west-coast coves are all unaffected.

> 🐢 **The pairing we'd suggest:** Navagio viewpoint and a north-coast boat trip on one day, a turtle-spotting cruise in the Gulf of Laganas on another. Between them you get the island's two genuinely world-class experiences, neither of which depends on the beach reopening. ## A Note on the Ship Itself

The wreck is the **MV Panagiotis**, a coaster that ran aground on 2 October 1980 in stormy weather and poor visibility. The popular version — that it was a smuggler's ship running contraband cigarettes, intercepted by the Greek navy — is widely repeated, including by tour operators, and is at best only partly established. Official sources did not confirm smuggling at the time and the captain was never convicted of it, though later-released court documents and photographs have lent the story more weight. What is documented is that 29 locals were convicted of looting cargo and equipment from the wreck after it grounded.

The ship has been sitting in the limestone gravel ever since, which is why the erosion-protection works matter: it's a rusting steel hull on an exposed shore, and it will not last indefinitely.


## Practical Tips for Visiting Shipwreck Beach

**Check the status before you fly.** The closure runs to 31 October 2026 and is reviewed roughly twice yearly. It has been extended every time so far — but verify rather than assume, in either direction.

**Do the viewpoint early.** 9–11am for light and crowds. After that in high summer you're queuing for the platform in full sun.

**Hire a car for at least one day.** The viewpoint, Porto Vromi, the mountain villages and the west-coast coves all sit in the same part of the island and make a natural loop that no single tour covers well.

**Book boat trips with free cancellation.** Wind cancels these regularly, and the cove is exposed. Never book Navagio for your last day.

**Read the tour listing carefully.** Anything promising time on the sand is out of date. "Photo stop from the water" is the honest wording.

**Don't fly a drone.** Not permitted without authorisation, and fined.

**Take cash.** Small harbours and the viewpoint kiosk don't all take cards, and parking may be a couple of euros.

**Wear closed shoes at the viewpoint.** Loose gravel, a cliff edge, and strong wind is a bad combination for flip-flops.

**Take seasickness tablets if you're prone.** The crossing to the cove is short but can be choppy — this coast is exposed to the open Ionian.
