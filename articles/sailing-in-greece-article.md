# Sailing in Greece: Routes, Costs and How to Plan Your Trip

**Subtitle:** Greece has more islands than any country in Europe, more coastline than France, and a wind pattern — the Meltemi — that fills sails reliably from June through September across the central Aegean. The Greek sailing tradition is one of the oldest in the world: the country's mythology is inseparable from the sea, its economy was built on it, and its landscape — 227 inhabited islands, 6,000 islands and islets in total, connected by channels, straits, and open water of extraordinary clarity — is designed for exploration by boat. If you have ever wanted to sail in the Mediterranean, Greece is where to do it.

**Excerpt:** Sailing in Greece divides into three broad types: skippered charters (someone else drives, you relax), bareboat charters (you drive your own yacht), and flotilla sailing (a fleet of yachts sailing together with a lead crew). This guide covers routes by island group, realistic costs for each sailing type, what the Meltemi wind means for route planning, and where to find the best day-trip sailing experiences if you want the sea without the commitment of a week-long charter.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The Ionian Islands are the best sailing destination for beginners and families.** Calm seas (the Meltemi doesn't blow here), short passages between islands (1–4 hours), reliable anchorages, and stunning scenery. Lefkada/Nidri is the primary Ionian charter base.
2. **The Cyclades offer the best classic sailing but require experience.** The Meltemi is a real wind — beautiful sailing when you use it correctly, genuinely uncomfortable when you fight it. The passages between islands can be rough. The reward is extraordinary: island hopping between Mykonos, Naxos, Paros, Santorini, and smaller islands in between.
3. **Skippered charters are the best value for non-sailors who want a sailing holiday.** You get a qualified captain, a crewed boat, and a week at sea without needing any sailing experience. The captain's local knowledge — where the best anchorages are, which route to take with the current wind — is worth as much as the boat.
4. **Day sailing trips are available everywhere in Greece and require no commitment.** A 5-hour catamaran cruise from Athens, Santorini, or Mykonos gives you the sailing experience, sea swimming, and Greek lunch on the water without booking a full week. These are excellent introductions to Greek sailing and genuinely enjoyable on their own terms.
5. **Book early for peak season (July-August).** Popular charter routes — particularly the Cyclades in summer — book out 3–6 months in advance. Shoulder season (May-June, September-October) has better prices, better weather for some sailors, and significantly easier booking.

## ARTICLE BODY

The Greek sailing tradition predates the written word. The ancient Minoans were trading across the Aegean 4,000 years ago. Odysseus sailed it (and had trouble with the gods along the way). The Phoenicians, the Romans, the Byzantines, and the Venetians all operated maritime networks across these waters. Contemporary Greece continues the tradition: it has the largest merchant fleet in the world relative to population, the most yacht charter companies in the Mediterranean, and a coastline that produces sailors the way Switzerland produces skiers.

What makes Greece exceptional for sailing is the combination of island density and wind reliability. The Meltemi is predictable: it blows from the same direction (north to northwest) at roughly the same strength for most of the summer. That predictability allows route planning in a way that the variable winds of the western Mediterranean do not. And the sheer number of islands — each with its own character, anchorages, and tavernas — means that a week of sailing in Greece is genuinely different from anywhere else.

This guide covers everything you need to know to plan a sailing trip to Greece, whether that means booking a full week's charter or spending a morning on a catamaran cruise from your hotel.


## The Meltemi: Greece's Sailing Wind

Before routes and costs, the Meltemi deserves its own section, because it shapes every sailing plan in the Aegean.

The Meltemi is a seasonal northerly wind originating from high-pressure systems over the Balkans and the Aegean. It blows most reliably from approximately mid-June to mid-September, most strongly in the central and northern Cyclades (Mykonos, Paros, Naxos) and the northern Aegean.

**Typical Meltemi characteristics:**
- Force 4–6 (15–25 knots) on most summer days
- Occasionally force 7–8 (up to 35+ knots) during stronger episodes
- Builds through the day (typically strongest in the afternoon) and eases at night
- Blows from the north or northwest — which means sailing north in the Cyclades means sailing into the wind, and sailing south means sailing with it

**What the Meltemi means for route planning:**
- Plan multi-day Cyclades routes to sail south (downwind) rather than north
- The morning (before 11am) is often calm; depart early for calmer conditions
- Some north-facing bays become untenable in strong Meltemi; know your anchorage alternatives
- The Ionian Islands (western Greece) are largely unaffected by the Meltemi — an important reason the Ionians are the preferred sailing ground for beginners

**What the Meltemi means for sailing experience:**
Good sailors find the Meltemi exhilarating — reliable breeze, spectacular sailing, warm dry air. Beginners can find it alarming. If you are new to sailing, start in the Ionian or visit in May-June before the Meltemi establishes.


## Greek Sailing Routes by Region

### The Ionian Islands: Best for Beginners and Families

The Ionian sailing circuit — typically based from Lefkada/Nidri or Corfu — is the finest introduction to Greek sailing. The inter-island passages are short (1–4 hours), the sea is calmer than the Aegean (the Meltemi doesn't blow here), the anchorages are reliable, and the scenery is extraordinary: green mountainous islands, Venetian-era ports, and the specific clarity of the Ionian Sea.

**Standard 7-day Ionian circuit from Lefkada:**

Day 1: Nidri/Lefkada → Meganisi (1 hour) — anchor in the beautiful Vathy bay
Day 2: Meganisi → Ithaca (2 hours, Vathy harbour) — Odysseus's home island
Day 3: Ithaca → Kefalonia (1.5 hours, Fiskardo) — the best preserved Venetian port in the Ionian
Day 4: Fiskardo → Ithaca → Astakos (mainland, 2.5 hours) — anchor in a quiet mainland harbour
Day 5: Astakos → Kalamos island (1 hour) — tiny island, excellent anchorage, no tourists
Day 6: Kalamos → Lefkada south coast (2 hours) — the dramatic west-coast cliffs
Day 7: Return to Nidri

**Alternative: The Ionian chain south from Lefkada:**
Lefkada → Kefalonia → Ithaca → Zakynthos (3 hours south) → return north. Extends to 10–14 days.

### The Cyclades: Classic Greek Island Sailing

The Cyclades circuit from Athens (Alimos Marina, Piraeus) is the most iconic Greek sailing route — island-hopping through the heart of the Aegean. The Meltemi makes this a more experienced sailor's territory, but the rewards are commensurate.

**Standard 7-day Cyclades circuit from Athens:**

Day 1: Athens/Piraeus → Kea (3.5 hours) — first Cycladic island, excellent anchorage at Ayios Nikolaos
Day 2: Kea → Syros (3 hours) — the Cyclades capital, magnificent neoclassical town of Ermoupoli
Day 3: Syros → Mykonos (2.5 hours) — enter the harbour by morning; anchor at Ornos Bay to avoid the crowds
Day 4: Mykonos → Delos → Paros (4 hours) — sail past the sacred island (anchor overnight at Naoussa or Paros)
Day 5: Paros → Naxos (1.5 hours) — the largest and most varied Cycladic island; sail around the coast
Day 6: Naxos → Ios (2 hours) — caldera-like bay, dramatic south-coast anchorages
Day 7: Ios → Syros → Athens (6–7 hours, typically motor-assisted north) — early morning departure

The southward route to Santorini (add 2–3 days: Ios → Sikinos → Santorini) is the classic extension.

### The Dodecanese: History and Remote Islands

The Dodecanese circuit, based from Rhodes or Kos, combines some of the finest historical sailing in Greece with access to smaller, less-visited islands.

**Standard route from Rhodes:**
Rhodes → Symi (2 hours) → Tilos (3 hours) → Nisyros (2 hours, sail into the caldera) → Kos (2 hours) → Kalymnos (30 min) → Leros (45 min) → Patmos (1.5 hours) → return south (or continue to Athens via overnight ferry).

The Dodecanese route is particularly rewarding in May-June and September-October, when the crowds at Rhodes and Kos are manageable and the smaller islands (Tilos, Nisyros, Lipsi) are at their most authentic.

### The Saronic Gulf: Athens Day Sailing

The Saronic Gulf — the body of water between Attica and the northeastern Peloponnese — is the sailing ground closest to Athens and ideal for 1–7 day trips from the city. The islands (Aegina, Agistri, Poros, Hydra, Spetses) are all accessible within a few hours.

The [Athens catamaran cruise](https://www.getyourguide.com/athens-l91/athens-riviera-half-day-catamaran-sailing-cruise-t119410/) along the Athens Riviera (5 hours, departing from Alimos Marina, with BBQ meal and drinks) is the most accessible way to experience sailing from Athens — the coastline between Alimos and Vouliagmeni is genuinely beautiful, and the swim stops in the clear water off the Athenian coast surprise most visitors who expect a city sea to be industrial.

For a full-day Saronic experience, the [Athens sailing cruise to Aegina](https://www.getyourguide.com/athens-l91/from-athens-full-day-boat-trip-to-aegina-island-with-lunch-t147195/) (full day, departing from Alimos Marina) sails to the Temple of Aphaia island with lunch on the water — the classic Athens-within-reach sailing day.

## Types of Sailing Holiday in Greece

### Bareboat Charter

A bareboat charter gives you a boat with no crew — you are the skipper. Requires a qualifying sailing qualification (RYA Day Skipper or equivalent nationally recognised licence, or demonstrable experience on a logbook). This is the most freedom-intensive sailing option: you plan your own route, make your own decisions, and experience the full independence of sailing.

**What you need:** A recognised sailing qualification, demonstrated sea miles, and ideally some Mediterranean or tidal sailing experience. Greek charter companies typically require a Day Skipper certificate minimum and may require additional qualifications for larger vessels or the more exposed routes.

**Costs (approximate, per week, excluding fuel, provisions, marina fees):**
- 36-38 foot monohull: €1,200–2,000 (May/October) | €1,800–3,000 (July-August)
- 42-45 foot monohull: €1,800–3,000 (May/October) | €2,500–4,500 (July-August)
- 40-42 foot catamaran: €2,500–4,000 (May/October) | €4,000–7,000 (July-August)

Add approximately €400–600 for provisions for a week for 6–8 people, €100–200 for marina fees (anchoring is free throughout Greece outside marinas), and €100–300 for diesel.

**Main bareboat charter companies:** Sunsail, Moorings, Dream Yacht Charters, Neilson — all operate multiple Greek bases. Direct local operators in Lefkada (Nidri) are often cheaper than international companies.

---

### Skippered Charter (Crewed Yacht)

A skippered charter provides a professional captain with the boat — you bring the crew (friends, family, a mixed group of passengers), and the skipper does the navigation, route planning, anchoring, and heavy work. You enjoy the sailing experience without requiring qualifications.

**What you need:** Nothing except to show up. The skipper manages everything.

**Costs:** A skippered charter costs approximately 25–40% more than a comparable bareboat, because the skipper's fee (typically €100–150/day) and any additional crew costs are added to the boat hire. For groups who are not qualified to sail, this is often the best value option — the skipper's knowledge of anchorages, weather windows, and local tavernas is worth the cost.

**Finding skippered charters:** Most Greek charter companies offer skippered options. Established platforms include Boatsetter, Click&Boat, and direct company websites.

---

### Flotilla Sailing

A flotilla is a fleet of yachts — typically 8–15 boats — sailing together on the same general route, with a lead boat crewed by professional flotilla leaders. The fleet follows a broadly shared itinerary, gathering in the same harbour each evening, with the lead crew available for advice, repairs, and sociable evening dinners.

Flotilla sailing is the ideal format for inexperienced sailors who want to learn, or for solo travellers who want a social sailing holiday without joining a stranger's crew. It combines independence (you sail your own boat, within the fleet's general framework) with support.

**Main flotilla operators in Greece:** Sunsail and Neilson operate the largest flotilla programmes in the Ionian Islands and Cyclades. First Choice sailing also operates Greek flotillas. Prices typically include accommodation in the yacht, the flotilla leader service, and some social events.

**Best routes for flotilla:** The Ionian (Lefkada-based) is the most popular flotilla region — the protected sea, short passages, and reliable anchorages are ideal for flotilla format.

## When to Sail in Greece

### May–June (Excellent)

The Meltemi has not fully established — winds are gentler and more variable, making these months ideal for inexperienced sailors and those who want calmer conditions. The sea temperature is 20–22°C — cool but swimmable. Fewer boats in the anchorages, lower charter prices. Wildflowers still on the hillsides.

### July–August (Classic but Demanding)

The Meltemi is at its strongest — force 4–6 most days, occasionally 7–8. Excellent sailing for experienced crews; challenging for beginners. Crowded anchorages at the most popular spots (Mykonos, Paros, Santorini). Highest charter prices. Book 4–6 months in advance.

### September–October (Best Overall)

The Meltemi eases significantly in September and is largely gone by October. The sea is at its warmest (24–26°C in September, 20–22°C in October). Fewer boats, lower prices, all facilities still open. October water temperatures cooler but still swimmable. The strongest argument for a shoulder-season sailing holiday in Greece.


## Practical Sailing Greece Guide

### Marinas and Anchorages

Greece has approximately 50 marinas of varying quality. Athens (Alimos, Flisvos), Corfu, Rhodes, Kos, and Lefkada have the most developed marina infrastructure. Outside marinas, anchoring is free throughout Greece — virtually every bay and cove can be anchored in, and many of the finest stops on a Greek sailing holiday are completely free wild anchorages.

A Greek sailing week might involve 2–3 marina nights (primarily for showers and provisioning) and 4–5 anchor nights (quieter, more atmospheric, completely free). The balance is a matter of preference.

### Provisioning

Supermarkets on the main islands are fully stocked and comparable to mainland European standards. Smaller islands have limited supplies — provision on the main island before heading to smaller stops. Greek island markets typically open 8am–2pm and reopen 5–8pm (siesta pattern).

### Safety and Weather

Greek coastguard (JRCC Greece): +30 210 411 2500. VHF Channel 16 for maritime emergency contact throughout Greek waters.

Weather forecasting: Poseidon (poseidon.hcmr.gr) is the official Greek meteorological service maritime forecast — reliable and freely available. Windguru and Windfinder are also widely used by Greek sailors.
