# Meze Culture in Greece: How to Eat Like a Greek

**Subtitle:** Meze is not a course in a meal. It is a way of eating — multiple small dishes shared across the centre of a table, over a long period of time, with drink alongside throughout. Understanding meze culture is understanding the social logic of Greek food, and it changes what a meal in Greece can be.

**Excerpt:** The word meze comes from the Turkish *meze* (itself from Persian *maza*, meaning taste or flavour). In Greece, it describes both individual dishes and an entire style of eating. You can order meze at a taverna, at an ouzerie, or at a tsipouradiko in Volos where the meze arrives free with every order of tsipouro. The principle is always the same: food and drink arrive slowly, nobody is in a hurry, and the meal continues as long as the company is good.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Meze is not appetisers.** The common description of mezedes as "Greek tapas" or "Greek appetisers" undersells what the format actually is. At a proper meze session, the dishes are the meal — there is no main course that follows, no expectation of a transition. You order meze, you eat meze, and when you are done, the meal is done.
2. **The drink comes first, the food follows.** In the traditional meze format, you order ouzo, tsipouro, or wine, and the food exists to accompany the drink — not the other way around. This is not about drinking heavily; it is about the pace, which is set by the glass rather than by the dish.
3. **Everything is shared.** This is the non-negotiable rule of the meze table. Individual portions exist in Greek food — but not at a meze. Common plates go in the centre; everyone reaches in. Using your own fork directly on the communal plate is standard and expected.
4. **The best meze experience in Greece is at a tsipouradiko in Volos.** The combination of small tsipouro bottles, automatically arriving meze plates, long tables, and the specific culture of that city produces a food and drink experience that is uniquely Greek and unlike anything available elsewhere in Europe.
5. **Meze is not a fixed list of dishes.** The specific dishes called meze vary by region, by season, and by the type of drink being accompanied. Ouzo meze at the coast is different from inland tsipouradiko meze, which is different from the mezedes at an Athens ouzerie. The principle is consistent; the specific dishes are variable.

## ARTICLE BODY

The Mediterranean eating tradition has been described many times as slow, social, and food-centred. Greek meze culture is its most fully realised expression. It is a format in which the purpose of sitting at a table with food and drink is not the nourishment but the company — the food is the structure around which a long, unhurried conversation takes place.

This guide covers what meze is, how it works, the different contexts in which you encounter it in Greece, what to order, and how to approach it as a visitor who wants to eat the way Greeks actually eat.

For the broader context of Greek food, see the [Greek food guide](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/greek-food-guide). For the practical guide to eating at a taverna, see [how to eat at a Greek taverna](https://greektriplanner.me/blog/greek-taverna).


## What Meze Is

The word *meze* (μεζές, plural *mezedes* or *mezeδes*, colloquially *mezedakia* for smaller versions) refers to small dishes designed for sharing. In Greek, it can mean a single dish — "ena meze" is a portion — but in practice it describes an entire mode of eating: multiple small dishes, ordered gradually, eaten slowly with drink alongside.

Meze is not appetisers. The difference matters because the appetiser exists to precede something — a main course, a dessert, a more substantial dish. The meze is the thing itself. At a Greek ouzerie or tsipouradiko, you do not order meze before the meal. The meze is the meal.

The Turkish origin of the word reflects the Ottoman-era food culture that shaped much of Greek cuisine — the meze tradition is shared across Turkey, Lebanon, Iran, and the broader Middle Eastern world, each with its own specific expression. The Greek version is built around the country's specific drinking culture: primarily ouzo, tsipouro, and wine.


## The Principle: Drink First, Food Second

The most important thing to understand about Greek meze culture is the relationship between the drink and the food. In most Western dining contexts, the food is the point and the drink is the accompaniment. In Greek meze culture, this is reversed.

You sit down. You order ouzo, tsipouro, or wine. The drink arrives. The food arrives to accompany the drink — to slow its absorption, to give the palate something to do between sips, and to provide a reason to remain at the table. More drink is ordered. More food arrives. The session continues.

This structure produces a meal that is fundamentally different from the standard three-course format. There is no starter/main/dessert sequence. There is no sense of moving through stages toward a conclusion. There is drink, there is food, there is company — and the meal ends when all three have run their natural course.

**The practical result:** At a proper meze session, you will eat less than you might expect, drink more slowly than you might expect, and spend considerably longer at the table than you might expect. This is not a failure of the format. It is the format.


## The Settings: Where Meze Happens

### The Ouzerie (Ουζερί)

The specialist meze restaurant, built around ouzo. The ouzerie arrived in its current form in the early 20th century, particularly in Athens, Thessaloniki, and the Aegean islands. You order a bottle or carafe of ouzo (or individual glasses, depending on the establishment), and mezedes arrive — either a fixed selection determined by the kitchen or chosen from a menu.

**How it works:** Sit down. Order ouzo — specify the brand if you have a preference, or ask what they have. The first meze will arrive: typically something simple — olives, a piece of feta, bread — followed by more substantial dishes as the session develops. You can order more or let the kitchen decide.

**The Athenian ouzerie:** The classic ouzerie in Athens tends to be in Monastiraki, Psirri, or along the coast toward Piraeus. The format is similar to a taverna but more specifically anchored to ouzo and meze rather than a full menu.

### The Tsipouradiko (Τσιπουράδικο) — Volos

The tsipouradiko is the defining institution of Greek meze culture and, arguably, one of the best food-and-drink experiences in Greece. Centred in the city of Volos on the Thessaly coast (and increasingly found elsewhere), a tsipouradiko operates on a specific economic principle: you pay for the tsipouro; the meze is complimentary.

A small bottle of tsipouro arrives (typically 200ml, around €4–6). With it comes a plate of meze — something simple, something salty, something to eat with the drink. When the bottle is finished, another arrives if you want it, and more meze arrives with it. The plates continue as long as the tsipouro continues. You pay at the end only for the tsipouro consumed.

**The dishes:** Tsipouradiko meze is typically more substantial than ouzerie meze and reflects the inland Thessaly tradition: grilled sausage, fried courgettes, small fish, cheese, spreads, pickled vegetables, sometimes more complex dishes as the evening progresses. The specific rotation varies by establishment and by the cook's mood that day.

**Why it works:** The economic model aligns the kitchen's incentives with the diner's experience in an unusual way. The better the meze, the more tsipouro you drink. This produces kitchens genuinely motivated to keep the quality of the food high.

A dedicated trip to Volos specifically to eat at a tsipouradiko is a defensible travel decision.

### The Fish Taverna Meze Table

At a coastal Greek fish taverna, the meze format adapts to the marine context. Grilled octopus, fried calamari, marinated anchovies, taramasalata, saganaki, and a horiatiki salad arrive for the table while the main fish is being prepared. Ouzo is the standard drink — the anise flavour is a specific complement to the salt and acidity of the seafood.

### The Home Table

At a Greek home, meze appears in its most informal and generous form. The table fills with dips, cheese, olives, bread, and a series of dishes that arrive from the kitchen over the course of an hour or more. Ouzo or tsipouro begins the meal; wine takes over as the food builds. This is the meze tradition in its original context, and encountering it at a Greek household — if you are fortunate enough to be invited — is the best version of the experience available.


## What to Order: Building a Meze Table

The composition of a good meze table follows a logic of contrast and progression — lighter things first, richer things later, with variety across textures and flavours throughout.

**Start with:**
- **Olives** — Kalamata or a regional variety, dressed with olive oil, lemon, and herbs
- **Feta** — a slab with olive oil poured over and dried oregano; bread alongside
- **Tzatziki** — the anchor dip of any meze table
- **Taramasalata** — the fish roe dip that is the essential companion to ouzo at the coast

**Add:**
- **Dolmadakia** — stuffed vine leaves at room temperature
- **Saganaki** — fried cheese, hot
- **Keftedes** — pan-fried meatballs, served warm
- **Grilled octopus** (at a coastal setting) — the classic ouzo companion
- **Fried calamari** — light, hot, squeezed with lemon

**Then:**
- **Kolokithokeftedes** (courgette fritters) — crispy outside, soft inside, excellent with tzatziki
- **Tomatokeftedes** (tomato fritters, if in season on Santorini) — intensely flavoured, unique
- **Bouyiourdi** — baked feta with tomatoes and peppers in a clay pot; rich and hot

**Finish with:**
- More olives, more bread, more of whatever was best — or nothing. The meze is done when the drink is done.


## The Etiquette of the Meze Table

**Fork directly into the common plate.** This is standard. Using a serving spoon is optional and equally acceptable, but reaching directly in with your own fork is not considered rude — it is how the table works.

**Order one dish at a time.** The meze format is designed for slow accumulation, not for ordering ten things simultaneously. Order two or three dishes, see what arrives, assess what is needed, order more. This is also how the kitchen can produce things at their best — hot when hot, cold when cold, in the right sequence.

**Do not rush the drink.** Meze is not about drinking quickly. A glass of ouzo properly prepared and drunk properly — small sips, a bite of food between, water alongside — can occupy twenty minutes. This is correct.

**Let dishes arrive at their pace.** Nothing is synchronized. The hot dishes come hot, the cold dishes are already there, the bread is continuous. Do not wait for everything to arrive before beginning. Eat as things come.

**Do not ask for the bill the moment the last dish is finished.** Order a coffee. Drink some water. Let the conversation finish naturally. The bill is asked for when you are genuinely ready to leave.


## Meze and Drink: The Correct Pairings

**Ouzo → coastal meze:** Grilled octopus, fried calamari, marinated anchovies, taramasalata, feta, olives. The anise of the ouzo and the salinity of seafood are a natural pair that has been refined over centuries.

**Tsipouro without anise → assertive meze:** Cured meats, aged cheese, pickled vegetables, grilled sausage. The grape character of unspiced tsipouro pairs with richer, more assertive flavours.

**Tsipouro with anise → similar to ouzo:** Works with the same seafood and cheese meze as ouzo but has more grape complexity underneath the anise.

**White wine (assyrtiko) → lighter meze:** Dolmadakia, lighter dips, fish meze, vegetable dishes. The acidity of a good assyrtiko cuts through the olive oil in lathera dishes and the richness of feta beautifully.

**Red wine (xinomavro) → heavier meze:** Keftedes, saganaki with a strong cheese, grilled meats. The tannin and acidity of xinomavro needs food with weight.


## FAQs

**What is meze in Greece?**

Meze (μεζές) refers to small dishes designed for sharing, eaten alongside ouzo, tsipouro, or wine over a long, unhurried period. It is not an appetiser course — at a Greek ouzerie or tsipouradiko, mezedes are the entire meal. The format is social and slow, with food and drink arriving gradually and the meal continuing as long as the company lasts.

**What are typical Greek meze dishes?**

The standard meze spread includes olives, feta with olive oil and oregano, tzatziki, taramasalata, dolmadakia (stuffed vine leaves), saganaki (fried cheese), keftedes (meatballs), and grilled octopus or fried calamari at the coast. Specific dishes vary by region and by what is in season.

**What is a Greek ouzerie?**

An ouzerie (ουζερί) is a specialist meze restaurant built around ouzo. You order ouzo and mezedes arrive — either a kitchen selection or from a menu. It is the classic Athenian and Aegean setting for the meze format. Similar to a tsipouradiko but centred on ouzo rather than tsipouro.

**What is a tsipouradiko?**

A tsipouradiko is a specialist tsipouro restaurant, primarily associated with the city of Volos in central Greece. The model is: you pay for small bottles of tsipouro; meze plates arrive complimentary and keep coming as long as you keep drinking. It is considered one of the best food-and-drink experiences in Greece.

**How do you eat meze properly?**

Order two or three dishes to start, eat from the shared common plates with your fork, let dishes arrive at their own pace, and refill drink and food gradually over the course of one to three hours. Do not try to synchronise courses. Do not rush the bill. Let the meal end when the conversation does.
